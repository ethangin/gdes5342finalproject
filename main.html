TOM BRADY

Career

This is Tom Brady: seven-time Super Bowl champion, five-time Super Bowl MVP, and one of the winningest quarterbacks of the modern era, just to name a few achievements. Whenever he decides to retire, he is certain to be a first-ballot Hall of Famer and an unforgettable legend.

But how did he get here, and what else has helped shape his story as he has become “The GOAT”?

2000

Tom Brady entered the NFL as a relatively unheralded quarterback. He was drafted by the New England Patriots in the sixth round with the 199th pick. His scouting report included downsides such as his size, physical build, and lack of mobility or a strong arm.

2001

Brady wouldn’t get a chance to start until 2001, when Patriots starter Drew Bledsoe suffered an injury in Week 1 (pictured above). Brady entered the game and never gave back the starting job. He would go on to lead the Patriots to an 11-5 record and an AFC East title.

2002

Brady led the Patriots to the Super Bowl in his first season as a starter, where they defeated the St. Louis Rams, known as the “Greatest Show On Turf”, by a score of 20-17 (pictured above). With a final stat line of 16/27 passes completed for 145 yards and a touchdown, Brady was named the Super Bowl MVP.

2004

Brady then led the Patriots to back-to-back Super Bowl victories in the 2003-04 and 2004-05 seasons, defeating Jake Delhomme’s Carolina Panthers in 2004 (pictured above) and Donovan McNabb’s Philadelphia Eagles in 2005. Brady also was named the Super Bowl MVP in 2004.

2008

The Patriots were having a historic season in 2008. With an explosive offense that was the best in the league and a top-10 scoring defense, the Patriots went undefeated in the regular season. It seemed like no one could stop them from winning the Super Bowl, either...until Eli Manning led the New York Giants to a stunning 17-14 victory, capped by David Tyree’s legendary “helmet catch” (above).

2008

Fresh off their Super Bowl loss to the Giants, the Patriots were ready to dominate the NFL again in the 2008-09 season. However, in Week 1, Tom Brady tore his ACL, which would sideline him for the entire season.

2012

After a few relatively quiet years, the Patriots returned to the Super Bowl in the 2011-2012 season after a 13-3 regular season. However, they were upset yet again by Eli Manning’s Giants after a critical series of catches by receiver Mario Manningham (above) to seal a comeback victory.

2015

In 2015, Tom Brady, coach Bill Belichick, and the Patriots came under fire for allegedly deflating footballs during the playoffs, making them easier to grip and throw (also known as Deflategate). Despite the controversy, he was allowed to proceed in the playoffs and the Super Bowl that season, although he would be suspended for the first four games of the next season.

2015

In the Super Bowl immediately after Deflategate started, the Patriots defeated the “Legion of Boom” Seattle Seahawks after a questionable playcall by the Seahawks to pass the ball from the Patriots 1-yard line instead of handing off to their star running back Marshawn Lynch. Instead, Malcolm Butler picked off Russell Wilson, leading to another Patriots Super Bowl victory and another MVP award for Tom Brady.

2017

Brady again led the Patriots to the Super Bowl in the 2016-17 season. After falling behind 28-3 to the Atlanta Falcons at halftime, the Patriots scored 31 unanswered points in the second half for a comeback victory and yet another Super Bowl MVP for Brady.

2018

The Patriots again made the Super Bowl during the 2017-18 season, as Brady sought his sixth Super Bowl victory. However, they were defeated by the Philadelphia Eagles and backup quarterback Nick Foles, leading to questions about if Brady was nearing the end of the career and how much he had left in the tank.

2019

The Patriots defeated the Los Angeles Rams in the Super Bowl of the 2018-19 season with a final score of 10-3. With both teams sporting excellent defenses, neither offense was able to make much progress. This only added to the questions about Brady’s ability to continue playing at a the high level expected of him.

2021

After the 2019-20 season, Brady left the Patriots in free agency and joined the Tampa Bay Buccaneers with his former star tight end Rob Gronkowski (pictured above), where he led the team to another Super Bowl victory over the Kansas City Chiefs and rising superstar Patrick Mahomes. His performance of 21/29 completions for 201 yards and 3 touchdowns, Brady won his fifth Super Bowl MVP and seventh Super Bowl Victory, both of which are easily the most in history for any player.

Comparison with Other QBs

Brady has had a great career so far, and, depending on who you ask, could potentially keep playing for quite a few more years.

But are there other factors that have allowed Brady to succeed and win when other great QBs have existed during his career? To answer this, it’s helpful to look at the numbers surrounding Brady, as well as the stats of three other Hall of Fame-caliber quarterbacks during Brady’s career: Peyton Manning, Drew Brees, and Aaron Rodgers.

Team Defense

Brady has benefitted from having a good to great defense behind him most years. For example, during the seven years where he won a Super Bowl, his defense ranked between the fifth and sixth best for points allowed, with that number decreasing to about the seventh best when considering all his years.

Compare that with Peyton Manning’s Colts from 2001-2011, Drew Brees’ Saints from 2006-2020, and Aaron Rodgers’ Packers from 2005-2020, who all have defenses that, on average, are middle-of-the-pack or worse.

Salary

Brady has also been willing to take pay cuts and restructure his contract many times. This has given his team a distinct advantage; on average, his teams were able to spend $1-3 million more on free agents than the other quarterbacks. This is also helped by the fact that his wife was one of the world’s richest supermodels for a long time, but his salary cap hit is significantly below other quarterbacks of his caliber, which has affected his teams significantly.




Conclusion

Tom Brady didn’t win seven Super Bowls by accident. He certainly deserves credit for his accomplishments. However, they are also partially a byproduct of the situations he has been fortunate enough to play in, whether that’s a great coach, a good defense behind him, or a willingness to take pay cuts.

At the same time, it also takes a great quarterback to win consistently in big moments and put up the statistics Brady has, not to mention his longevity.

Ultimately, Brady’s successes and situation are innseparable. They are both an integral part of his story, and his legacy is all the more legendary for it.
